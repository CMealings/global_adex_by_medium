Advertising expenditure in Asia Pacific stood at US$ 156.1bn in 2016, according to data from WARC. This represents year-on-year growth of 5.3% from 2015, when total spend amounted to US$ 148.3bn. Since 2007, Asia Pacific has registered year-on-year growth in advertising spend every year with the exception of 2015. However, this dip in 2015 can be attributed to a particularly strong dollar rather than an actual decline in adspend. The equivalent year-on-year comparison shown in PPPs (purchasing power parities)* indicates total regional adspend growth of 4.7% between 2014 and 2015.

When considering advertising expenditure by media channel in Asia Pacific, data show that TV has consistently accounted for the majority share of total adspend. In 2016, TV amounted to US$ 58.2bn, equivalent to a 37.3% share. Newspapers (US$ 17.2bn, 11.0% share) came next, followed by out of home (US$ 12.9bn, 8.3% share), radio (US$ 6.8bn, 4.4% share), magazines (US$ 3.9bn, 2.5% share) and lastly cinema (US$ 1.4bn, 0.9% share).

Back in 2007, newspapers was the second biggest channel for advertisers, when it took US$ 23.5bn, equivalent to a 25.3% share of total adspend. However, the channel has seen a dramatic decline in recent years, and share of adspend now stands at well under half its 2007 total. Newspapers accounted for just a 11.0% share of total adspend in 2016. Mobile and desktop internet have been the main beneficiaries of this fall, up 17.1pp and 10.8pp respectively.

* PPP = Purchasing Power Parity (PPPs) measures the buying power of local currency units when spent in their domestic markets. They enable accurate international comparisons of economies and prices by removing the impact of exchange rate fluctuations. The US economy is used as the global reference point for PPPs and is set to an index value of 100.
